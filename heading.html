Headings of HTML



In Html HEADINGS are used to define the structure and hierarchy of your content. They range from <h1> to <h6>, with <h1> being the highest (most important) level and <h6> being the lowest.
They are typically used for titles, subtitles, and sections to organize content in a way that is both user-friendly and SEO-friendly.


Let’s go through them in more detail:

  1. <h1> - Main Heading (Most Important)
    • Purpose: Used for the primary heading or title of a page or section.
    • Importance: It is considered the most important heading for search engine optimization (SEO), and it should describe the main content of the page.
  2. <h2> - Subheading (Second Level)
    • Purpose: Used for subsections within a main section.
    • Importance: It represents a secondary level of importance. It’s used for major sections that are below the main heading.
  3. <h3> - Subheading (Third Level)
    • Purpose: Used for further subdivisions under an <h2> heading.
    • Importance: It’s generally used for subtopics within a subsection.
  4. <h4> - Subheading (Fourth Level)
    • Purpose: Used for finer subdivisions under an <h3> heading.
  5. <h5> - Subheading (Fifth Level)
    • Purpose: Used for even more specific topics under <h4>.
  6. <h6> - Subheading (Sixth Level)
    • Purpose: This is the lowest-level heading. It’s generally used for fine details or smaller subtopics within an <h5>.

Key Points to remember

  • Hierarchy and Structure: The <h1> should be the first and most important heading on a page, and subsequent headings (<h2> to <h6>) should be used to create a clear hierarchy for the content.

  • SEO Considerations: Search engines like Google use these headings to understand the structure and relevance of the content on a webpage. So, it’s important to use the appropriate heading levels rather than skipping them or using them purely for styling purposes.

  • Styling: By default, headings are styled with bold text and larger font sizes. You can modify the appearance of headings using CSS, but they will always retain their semantic importance for SEO and accessibility purposes.
